Eyewitness Identification: What’s New in Police ID Procedures?
Dr. Dan Reisberg, Department of Psychology, Reed College

In the last few years, there have been huge changes in how Oregon police collect I.D. evidence and how courts evaluate this evidence. Many of those changes are reflected in new Department of Public Safety Standards and Training (DPSST) protocols — including a new DPSST training video. We’ll consider what’s good about the new protocols (and there’s a lot that’s good), and what’s worrisome. We’ll also consider the implications that recent court rulings have for how police collect and document identification evidence.

1:10

Break

1:20

Eyewitness Identification: What's New in the Law?
Aliza B. Kaplan, Associate Professor, Lewis & Clark Law School

In November 2012, a unanimous Oregon Supreme Court issued State v. Lawson, a landmark decision requiring major changes in the way courts are required to evaluate eyewitness identification evidence. The new legal framework is based on 30 years of scientific research that changed our understanding of memory and perception. Under Lawson, law enforcement, lawyers, and courts in Oregon must be able to understand the impact of suggestion on memory and the significance of suggestion in the admissibility of eyewitness testimony. We will consider Lawson, how it changed the law, and what has happened in the two years since Lawson. We will also consider what is yet to come—how Lawson might apply in specific situations and how we can use Lawson in practice.
